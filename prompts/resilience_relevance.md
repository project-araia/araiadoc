Judge whether this document is relevant to climate, weather, or environmental hazards and their
risks, impacts, resilience, adaptation, preparedness, response, recovery, planning, policy, governance,
public health, community, infrastructure, or ecosystem implications.

Definitions (as used in this context):
- Climate: the weather and environmental conditions affecting people, communities, infrastructure, and ecosystems.
- Hazard: an environmental circumstance or event that can harm people, communities, infrastructure, and ecosystems.
- Resilience: the ability of communities, infrastructure, or ecosystems to withstand, recover from, or adapt to a weather/climate/environmental hazard.
- Adaptation: deliberate adjustments by people, communities, or systems in response to actual or expected weather/climate/environmental hazards.
- Vulnerability: the degree to which people, communities, or systems are susceptible to harm from a weather/climate/environmental hazard.
- Exposure: the presence of people, infrastructure, or ecosystems in locations subject to a weather/climate/environmental hazard.
- Risk: the likelihood or magnitude of harm to people, systems, or ecosystems from a weather/climate/environmental hazard. Risk in finance, engineering reliability, or medicine does not qualify.
- Mitigation: reducing harm or exposure from a weather/climate/environmental hazard (e.g., flood-proofing, heat action plans). General emissions reduction or carbon policy qualifies only when linked to hazard impacts. Reducing noise, costs, or business risks does not qualify.
- Sustainability: long-term management of communities, infrastructure, or ecosystems in the face of weather/climate/environmental hazards. General environmental, economic, or corporate sustainability without a clear hazard link does not qualify.
- Governance: institutional or policy arrangements for managing weather/climate/environmental hazard risk. Corporate governance, data governance, or AI governance do not qualify.
- Displacement: forced movement of people caused by a weather/climate/environmental hazard (e.g., flood evacuation, wildfire evacuation). Displacement in signal processing or structural mechanics does not qualify.

THE CORE TEST — apply this first, before anything else. A document is RELEVANT only if BOTH are true:

1. HAZARD: it substantively involves at least one weather, climate, or environmental hazard (see list below), AND
2. CONTEXT: it substantively involves at least one impact, risk, exposure, vulnerability, resilience,
  adaptation, preparedness, response, recovery, planning, policy, governance, public-health, community,
  infrastructure, or ecosystem context (see list below).

If EITHER the hazard or the context is missing, weak, or only mentioned in passing, the document is NOT relevant (mark `maybe` or `irrelevant`).
**Do not judge relevance from isolated keywords.**

- extreme cold, cold waves, cold spells, freeze events, winter storms, frost, snowstorms, ice storms, blizzards

- drought, water scarcity, hydrologic drought, agricultural drought, meteorological drought, snow drought
- wildfire, forest fire, bushfire, fire weather, wildland fire, wildfire smoke, smoke exposure

Impact, risk, and response contexts include:

- climate risk, weather risk, hazard exposure, vulnerability, resilience, adaptation, mitigation, preparedness, emergency response, disaster recovery — all as defined above
- community impacts, social vulnerability, environmental justice, public health, mortality, morbidity, heat illness, smoke exposure, displacement (forced movement by a hazard), household or regional impacts
- infrastructure impacts or planning, including transportation, water systems, buildings, housing, communications, energy systems, public services, coastal assets, or urban systems
- ecosystem impacts, ecosystem services, forests, coasts, agriculture, water resources, biodiversity, natural-resource management, or climate-driven crop failure or yield loss
- planning, policy, governance (institutional arrangements for hazard risk), sustainability (long-term management under hazard conditions), disaster management, risk assessment, forecasting, early warning, decision support, or long-term adaptation strategies

Decision and score — these **MUST agree**. Choose the score first, then set `decision` from it:

- score 3 -> decision "relevant": Strongly relevant. A weather/climate/environmental hazard AND its impact/risk/resilience/planning context are the central focus.
- score 2 -> decision "relevant": Relevant. The hazard-and-context connection is clear and substantive but secondary to a broader topic (e.g., a multi-hazard review where one hazard is covered well, or a methods paper whose case study is a real hazard-impact context).
- score 1 -> decision "maybe": Partial or indirect. A relevant hazard but weak context, OR a strong resilience/adaptation/risk context but a vague or absent hazard link. Potentially useful but underdeveloped.
- score 0 -> decision "irrelevant": No meaningful hazard-and-context content.

**Never pair a high score with "irrelevant" or a 0 score with "relevant".**

Common false positives to mark IRRELEVANT unless they explicitly connect to the target hazard-and-impact/resilience topic:

- Papers that mention climate or weather only as background motivation but focus on unrelated methods, materials, chemistry, biology, physics, or computing.
- Pure atmospheric, oceanographic, hydrologic, or ecological mechanism studies with no meaningful risk, impact, vulnerability, adaptation, planning, response, policy, public-health, community, infrastructure, or ecosystem-services context.
- General sustainability, energy, agriculture, urban, or public-health papers with no clear weather/climate/environmental hazard component.
- Biomedical, genomics, clinical, pharmaceutical, pathogen, spectroscopy, astrophysics, high-energy physics, fusion/plasma, semiconductor fabrication, nanomaterials, or chemical-engineering papers unrelated to hazards and impacts.
- Documents where words like "storm," "heat," "flood," "risk," "resilience," or "adaptation" are used only metaphorically or in an unrelated technical sense.
- Documents driven by demographic, economic, or operational stressors (e.g., population growth, aging infrastructure, budget constraints) where a weather/climate/environmental hazard is listed alongside those stressors but never modeled, analyzed, or substantively discussed.

Important judging guidance:

- **When uncertain between two scores, pick the LOWER one. Prefer precision over recall.**
- Positive descriptive test: ask "does this document describe what a weather/climate/environmental hazard does to people, systems, ecosystems, or institutions?"
  If yes, the hazard is real here. If the hazard is only cited as motivation and the rest of the document could proceed without it, the hazard is not substantive -> not relevant.
- Judge only from the provided evidence. Sectionized text may omit references and some details; **do not assume content that is not shown.**
- A document can be relevant even if it is about policy, governance, economics, health, ecosystems, or planning rather than physical hazard modeling,
  as long as the weather/climate/environmental hazard connection is clear.
- A document can focus on a specific sector or system, but the relevance must come from the hazard-and-context connection,
  not the sector alone (e.g., "energy", "agriculture", "urban", "health" by itself is not enough).
- If the title/abstract are vague but multiple sections substantively discuss hazard-and-context content, mark relevant.
- If only one weak sentence mentions the target topic and the rest is unrelated, mark irrelevant.
