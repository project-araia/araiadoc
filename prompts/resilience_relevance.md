Judge whether this document is relevant to climate, weather, or environmental hazards and their risks, impacts, resilience, adaptation, preparedness, response, recovery, planning, policy, governance, public health, community, infrastructure, or ecosystem implications.

Goal: keep documents useful for studying how weather/climate/environmental hazards affect people, communities, infrastructure, ecosystems, planning, governance, public health, or sustainability, and how societies or systems prepare for, respond to, recover from, adapt to, or mitigate those hazards.

THE CORE TEST — apply this first, before anything else. A document is RELEVANT only if BOTH are true:

1. HAZARD: it substantively involves at least one weather, climate, or environmental hazard (see list below), AND
2. CONTEXT: it substantively involves at least one impact, risk, exposure, vulnerability, resilience, adaptation, preparedness, response, recovery, planning, policy, governance, public-health, community, infrastructure, or ecosystem context (see list below).

If EITHER the hazard or the context is missing, weak, or only mentioned in passing, the document is NOT relevant (mark `maybe` or `irrelevant`). Do not judge relevance from isolated keywords.

Weather, climate, and environmental hazard examples include:

- extreme heat, heat waves, heat mortality, wet-bulb temperature, heat stress, high temperatures, heat index, urban heat islands, urban warming
- extreme cold, cold waves, cold spells, freeze events, winter storms, frost, snowstorms, ice storms, blizzards
- floods, flash floods, river floods, urban flooding, coastal flooding, compound flooding, inundation, storm surge, heavy precipitation, pluvial flooding, riverine flooding, extreme rainfall
- drought, water scarcity, hydrologic drought, agricultural drought, meteorological drought, snow drought
- wildfire, forest fire, bushfire, fire weather, wildland fire, wildfire smoke, smoke exposure
- tropical cyclones, hurricanes, typhoons, cyclonic storms
- convective storms, severe thunderstorms, hail, straight-line winds, downbursts, microbursts, tornadoes, extreme wind
- sea level rise, coastal erosion, salinization, saltwater intrusion, ocean warming, marine heatwaves, ocean acidification
- sea ice loss, glacial melt, permafrost thaw, snowmelt timing, arctic sea ice change
- climate-related crop failure, crop yield effects, ecosystem service disruption

Impact, risk, and response contexts include:

- climate risk, weather risk, hazard exposure, vulnerability, resilience, adaptation, mitigation, preparedness, emergency response, disaster recovery
- community impacts, social vulnerability, environmental justice, public health, mortality, morbidity, heat illness, smoke exposure, displacement, household or regional impacts
- infrastructure impacts or planning, including transportation, water systems, buildings, housing, communications, energy systems, public services, coastal assets, or urban systems
- ecosystem impacts, ecosystem services, forests, coasts, agriculture, water resources, biodiversity, or natural-resource management
- planning, policy, governance, sustainability, disaster management, risk assessment, forecasting, early warning, decision support, or long-term adaptation strategies

Decision and score — these MUST agree. Choose the score first, then set `decision` from it:

- score 3 -> decision "relevant": Strongly relevant. A target hazard AND its impact/risk/resilience/planning context are the central focus.
- score 2 -> decision "relevant": Relevant. Clear substantive hazard-and-context content, but not the main focus or confined mainly to one major section.
- score 1 -> decision "maybe": Partial or indirect. A relevant hazard but weak context, OR resilience/adaptation/risk but a vague hazard link. Potentially useful but underdeveloped.
- score 0 -> decision "irrelevant": No meaningful hazard-and-context content.

Never pair a high score with "irrelevant" or a 0 score with "relevant". If you are torn between two scores, choose the LOWER one.

Common false positives to mark IRRELEVANT unless they explicitly connect to the target hazard-and-impact/resilience topic:

- Papers that mention climate or weather only as background motivation but focus on unrelated methods, materials, chemistry, biology, physics, or computing.
- Pure atmospheric, oceanographic, hydrologic, or ecological mechanism studies with no meaningful risk, impact, vulnerability, adaptation, planning, response, policy, public-health, community, infrastructure, or ecosystem-services context.
- General sustainability, energy, agriculture, urban, or public-health papers with no clear weather/climate/environmental hazard component.
- Biomedical, genomics, clinical, pharmaceutical, pathogen, spectroscopy, astrophysics, high-energy physics, fusion/plasma, semiconductor fabrication, nanomaterials, or chemical-engineering papers unrelated to hazards and impacts.
- Documents where words like "storm," "heat," "flood," "risk," "resilience," or "adaptation" are used only metaphorically or in an unrelated technical sense.

Important judging guidance:

- When uncertain between two scores, pick the LOWER one. Prefer precision over recall.
- Quick metaphor/keyword test: if removing the hazard word leaves the document's actual topic unchanged, the hazard is not real here -> not relevant.
- Judge only from the provided evidence. Sectionized text may omit references and some details; do not assume content that is not shown.
- A document can be relevant even if it is about policy, governance, economics, health, ecosystems, or planning rather than physical hazard modeling, as long as the weather/climate/environmental hazard connection is clear.
- A document can focus on a specific sector or system, but the relevance must come from the hazard-and-context connection, not the sector alone (e.g. "energy", "agriculture", "urban", "health" by itself is not enough).
- If the title/abstract are vague but multiple sections substantively discuss hazard-and-context content, mark relevant.
- If only one weak sentence mentions the target topic and the rest is unrelated, mark maybe or irrelevant depending on usefulness.

Examples (decision / score / why):

- "Analysis of Drought Vulnerability Characteristics and Risk Assessment in Southwest China" -> relevant / 3. Hazard (drought) + context (vulnerability, risk assessment) are the central focus.
- "Feedstock Recycling via Waste Plastic Pyrolysis" -> irrelevant / 0. Recycling/sustainability with no weather/climate/environmental hazard.
- "Synthesis and Characterization of Nanocrystalline Powders for Solid Oxide Fuel Cells" -> irrelevant / 0. Materials chemistry; energy is mentioned only as application, no hazard or impact context.
- "Cost-effectiveness of Dialysis Referral after Renal Transplant Failure" -> irrelevant / 0. Clinical/biomedical; no hazard ("failure" is unrelated technical usage).
- "Effects of crop residue and nitrogen levels on wheat grain yield" -> maybe / 1. Agriculture, but no weather/climate hazard is the driver; relevant only if it ties yield to drought/heat/flood, etc.
