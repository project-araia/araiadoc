
# Please spot-check the definitions in the above prompt. Official sources to back up these definitions would be appreciated.

Your definitions are broadly consistent with IPCC/UNDRR usage, with a few places where the wording could be tightened or where you are intentionally narrowing scope. Below is a spot‑check against standard references and suggested tweaks.

***

## Core climate risk terms

### Climate

Your definition
> “the weather and environmental conditions affecting people, communities, infrastructure, and ecosystems. Includes temperature, air pressure, humidity, precipitation…”

IPCC usage: “Climate” is usually defined more technically as the statistics of weather (mean and variability) over 30+ years, but in practice many policy and screening tools use a simplified, impact-oriented version like yours. IPCC AR6 and related guidance then distinguish “climate system,” “climate variability,” and “climate change.”[^1]

Assessment

- Conceptually fine for your purpose (screening for hazard–impact relevance).
- If you want closer alignment with IPCC, you might add “over time” or “long-term patterns of weather and environmental conditions.”

Example revision (if desired):
“Climate: the long‑term patterns of weather and environmental conditions (such as temperature, humidity, and precipitation) that affect people, communities, infrastructure, and ecosystems.”

***

### Hazard

Your definition
> “an environmental circumstance, event, danger, threat, or risk that can harm people, communities, infrastructure, and ecosystems.”

UNDRR definition:
> “A process, phenomenon or human activity that may cause loss of life, injury or other health impacts, property damage, social and economic disruption or environmental degradation.”[^2]

Assessment

- Very close in meaning.
- You might remove “risk” from your list of synonyms (since in IPCC/UNDRR usage hazard and risk are related but distinct concepts).

Suggested tweak:
“Hazard: an environmental circumstance, process, event, or threat that can harm people, communities, infrastructure, or ecosystems.”

***

### Resilience

Your definition
> “the ability of communities, infrastructure, or ecosystems to withstand, recover from, adjust, or adapt to a weather/climate/environmental hazard.”

IPCC AR6 (for climate‑resilient development, resilience builds on similar language): climate resilience is “the capacity of social, economic and ecosystems to cope with a hazardous event or trend or disturbance,” including the ability to reorganize, adapt, and learn.[^3]
Earlier IPCC reports define resilience as “the ability of a system…to anticipate, reduce, accommodate, or recover from the effects of a hazardous event or trend in a timely and efficient manner.”[^4]

Assessment

- Strongly aligned with IPCC and mainstream usage.
- Your focus on “withstand, recover, adjust, or adapt” captures the same ideas; adding “cope” or “reorganize” would bring it even closer, but is not essential.

***

### Adaptation

Your definition
> “deliberate adjustments by people, communities, or systems in response to actual or expected weather/climate/environmental hazards.”

IPCC definition (widely quoted via OECD/IPCC glossaries):
> “Adjustment in natural or human systems in response to actual or expected climatic stimuli or their effects, which moderates harm or exploits beneficial opportunities.”[^5][^6]

Assessment

- Fully consistent conceptually.
- You emphasize “deliberate,” whereas IPCC distinguishes “autonomous” vs. “planned” adaptation; for a relevance screen, restricting to deliberate adjustments is a reasonable pragmatic choice as long as that’s intentional.

If you want to hew closer to IPCC, you could drop “deliberate,” or clarify that you mainly care about planned/intentional actions.

***

### Vulnerability

Your definition
> “the degree to which people, communities, or systems are susceptible to harm from a weather/climate/environmental hazard.”

IPCC AR6:
> “Vulnerability describes the propensity or predisposition to be adversely affected. Vulnerability encompasses sensitivity or susceptibility to harm and lack of capacity to cope and adapt.”[^7][^1]

Assessment

- Very close and appropriate for your task.
- If you want more precision, you might add “propensity or predisposition” or mention limited coping/adaptive capacity, but it is not strictly necessary.

***

### Exposure

Your definition
> “the presence of people, infrastructure, or ecosystems in locations subject to a weather/climate/environmental hazard.”

IPCC AR6 / EEA:
> “Exposure describes the presence of people, livelihoods, species or ecosystems, environmental functions, services, and resources, infrastructure, or economic, social, or cultural assets in places and settings that could be adversely affected.”[^1]

Assessment

- Very well aligned.
- You are slightly narrower (you omit “livelihoods, services, assets”), which is fine for a screening tool.

***

### Risk

Your definition
> “the likelihood or magnitude of harm to people, systems, or ecosystems from a weather/climate/environmental hazard. Risk in finance, engineering reliability, or medicine does not qualify.”

IPCC AR6:
> “Risks are potential adverse consequences for human or ecological systems, recognizing the diversity of values and objectives associated with such systems.”[^1]
Climate risk is “the potential for negative consequences for society or ecosystems from the impacts of climate change.”[^7]

Assessment

- Substantively consistent, and your exclusion of non‑climate domains (finance, etc.) is appropriate for your relevance test.
- If you wanted to be closer to IPCC framing, you could say “potential for adverse consequences” rather than “likelihood or magnitude,” but your phrasing is still compatible.

***

### Mitigation

Your definition
> “reducing harm or exposure from a weather/climate/environmental hazard (e.g., flood-proofing, heat action plans). General emissions reduction or carbon policy qualifies only when linked to hazard impacts…”

IPCC definition of climate mitigation:
> “Mitigation… refers to human interventions to reduce emissions or enhance the sinks of greenhouse gases.”[^6]

In the disaster risk reduction (DRR) literature, “mitigation” is sometimes used more broadly for measures that reduce hazard impacts, but IPCC tries to keep “mitigation” for emissions and “risk reduction” or “disaster risk reduction” for impact‑side measures.

Assessment

- You are intentionally using a DRR‑style, impact‑reduction definition, not the IPCC emissions‑focused one.
- That is internally coherent for a hazard‑impact screening task, but it diverges from IPCC terminology, where your examples (flood‑proofing, heat plans) would usually fall under “adaptation” or “risk reduction,” not “mitigation.”[^6]

If you care about close IPCC alignment, you could:

- Either rename this to “risk mitigation” or “impact mitigation,” or
- Narrow it to “emissions mitigation” and treat “flood‑proofing, heat action plans” as “adaptation.”

***

### Sustainability

Your definition
> “long-term management of communities, infrastructure, or ecosystems in the face of weather/climate/environmental hazards. General environmental, economic, or corporate sustainability without a clear hazard link does not qualify.”

Mainstream definitions (e.g., sustainable development, climate‑resilient development) emphasize integrating mitigation, adaptation, and development to meet present needs without compromising future ones. Climate‑resilient development is defined as “a process of implementing greenhouse gas mitigation and adaptation measures to support sustainable development for all.”[^8]

Assessment

- You are deliberately narrowing “sustainability” to cases where there is an explicit hazard context, which is a sensible scoping rule for your relevance judgment.
- The definitional core (“long‑term management…” under hazard conditions) is compatible with broader sustainable development concepts, just more specific.

***

### Governance

Your definition
> “institutional or policy arrangements for managing weather/climate/environmental hazard risk. Corporate governance, data governance, or AI governance do not qualify.”

This aligns with how “governance” is used in IPCC WGII chapters on risk management, adaptation, and climate‑resilient development, where it refers to “institutional arrangements, decision processes, and power relations” around climate risk and adaptation.[^8]

Assessment

- Consistent in spirit with the IPCC literature.
- Your exclusion of corporate/data/AI governance is a clear and reasonable task‑specific narrowing.

***

### Displacement

Your definition
> “forced movement of people caused by a weather/climate/environmental hazard (e.g., flood evacuation, wildfire evacuation).”

The climate‑migration and disaster‑risk literature typically defines displacement as “forced movement of people from their homes or places of habitual residence, in particular as a result of or in order to avoid the effects of armed conflict, situations of generalized violence, violations of human rights, or natural or human‑made disasters.” Climate‑related displacement is then specifically tied to hazard events and climate impacts, as in IPCC WGII discussions of “displacement and migration” under climate risks.[^7]

Assessment

- Your definition fits standard usage of climate‑related displacement, just narrowed to hazards (which is what you want).
- The exclusion of technical uses (signal processing, mechanics) is a practical screening rule.

***

## Overall judgment and where to tweak

- Climate, hazard, resilience, adaptation, vulnerability, exposure, risk, and displacement are all well aligned with IPCC/UNDRR practice for the purposes of a relevance‑screening rubric.[^4][^2][^6][^1][^7]
- Sustainability and governance are intentionally narrowed to “sustainability/governance under climate or environmental hazard conditions,” which is reasonable for excluding generic ESG or management papers.
- The main terminological divergence from IPCC is “mitigation,” where your definition follows DRR usage (reducing harm from hazards) rather than the IPCC “emissions reduction” sense.[^6]

If you want to be maximally close to IPCC wording while keeping your screening gates, the two most useful edits would be:

1. Clarify mitigation as “impact or risk mitigation” or shift emissions‑oriented content to “mitigation” and your examples to “adaptation / risk reduction.”
2. Optionally add one IPCC phrase to resilience, vulnerability, and exposure (e.g., “propensity or predisposition to be adversely affected” for vulnerability) to make the linkage to official glossaries explicit.[^4][^1][^7]
