Judge whether this scientific document is relevant to climate, weather, or environmental hazards and their effects on people, communities, infrastructure, or ecosystems.

Definitions (aligned with IPCC AR6, UNDRR, and WMO terminology):
- Climate: the statistical characterization of atmospheric and environmental conditions (temperature, precipitation, humidity, pressure, wind) affecting human systems, infrastructure, and ecosystems over time.
- Hazard: a process, phenomenon, or condition with the potential to cause loss of life, injury, adverse health effects, property damage, infrastructure disruption, or ecosystem degradation (UNDRR, 2017).
- Resilience: the capacity of interconnected social, economic, and ecological systems to cope with a hazardous event by responding, reorganizing, and recovering while retaining essential function, identity, and structure (adapted from IPCC AR6 WGII).
- Adaptation: the process of adjustment in natural or human systems in response to actual or expected climatic stimuli or their effects, which moderates harm or exploits beneficial opportunities (IPCC AR6 WGII).
- Vulnerability: the propensity or predisposition of a system or population to be adversely affected by climate-related or environmental hazards, encompassing sensitivity and lack of adaptive capacity (IPCC AR6 WGII).
- Exposure: the presence of people, livelihoods, infrastructure, or ecosystems in places and settings that could be adversely affected by a hazard (IPCC AR6 WGII).
- Risk: the potential for adverse consequences arising from the interaction of hazard, exposure, and vulnerability. Risk in finance, engineering reliability, or clinical medicine does not qualify unless linked to a weather, climate, or environmental hazard.
- Mitigation (climate): efforts to reduce or prevent greenhouse gas emissions, or to enhance carbon sinks (IPCC AR6 WGIII). Mitigation (disaster risk): structural and non-structural measures undertaken to limit the adverse impact of natural hazards (UNDRR). Both senses qualify when linked to weather, climate, or environmental hazards. Reducing noise, costs, or business risks does not qualify.
- Sustainability: the long-term management of social, ecological, and infrastructure systems under present and projected climate and environmental conditions. General environmental, economic, or corporate sustainability without a clear hazard or climate link does not qualify.
- Governance: institutional, legal, and policy frameworks for managing weather, climate, or environmental hazard risk. Corporate governance, data governance, or AI governance do not qualify.
- Displacement: the forced movement of people due to weather, climate, or environmental hazards (e.g., flood evacuation, wildfire evacuation). Displacement in signal processing, structural mechanics, or fluid dynamics does not qualify.

THE CORE TEST — a document is RELEVANT if it substantively addresses ANY of the following:

1. A weather, climate, or environmental hazard (see list below).
2. The impacts, risks, vulnerability, or exposure of people, communities, infrastructure, or ecosystems to such hazards.
3. Resilience, adaptation, preparedness, response, recovery, mitigation, or planning related to such hazards.
4. Policy, governance, or institutional arrangements for managing climate/weather/environmental risk.

A document qualifies if it substantively addresses ANY ONE of the above — it does NOT need to cover both a hazard and its impacts simultaneously. A paper focused on drought modeling, flood forecasting, heat wave characterization, wildfire behavior, or sea level projections is relevant even without an explicit impact/resilience discussion. Similarly, a paper on community resilience planning, climate adaptation policy, or infrastructure vulnerability is relevant even if it discusses hazards at a general level.

**Do not judge relevance from isolated keywords.**

Weather, climate, and environmental hazard categories:

Heat-related:
- extreme heat: sustained periods of anomalously high temperature exceeding regional climatological norms, posing risks to human health, infrastructure, and ecosystems.
- heat waves: prolonged episodes (typically >= 3 consecutive days) of excessive heat, often combined with elevated humidity, exceeding defined thresholds for a given region.
- heat-related mortality: deaths directly or indirectly attributable to exposure to extreme ambient temperatures.
- wet-bulb temperature: a thermodynamic measure integrating air temperature and humidity, representing the theoretical lower limit of evaporative cooling. Sustained wet-bulb temperatures above ~35C indicate conditions beyond human thermoregulatory capacity.
- heat stress: the physiological or agronomic strain resulting from thermal loads that exceed the adaptive capacity of organisms, crops, or materials.
- elevated temperature: ambient temperatures above regional norms that increase morbidity, reduce system performance, or degrade ecosystem function.
- heat index: a composite metric of air temperature and relative humidity indicating the apparent temperature experienced by the human body.
- urban heat islands: the phenomenon whereby urbanized areas exhibit higher surface and air temperatures than surrounding rural areas due to modified land surfaces, reduced vegetation, and anthropogenic heat release.
- urban warming: long-term increases in urban ambient temperature driven by land-use change, impervious surface expansion, and reduced evapotranspiration.

Cold-related:
- extreme cold: anomalously low temperatures that exceed regional cold thresholds, with potential to cause hypothermia, infrastructure failure, and ecological damage.
- cold waves: multi-day episodes of abnormally low temperature relative to regional climatology.
- cold spells: shorter-duration periods of below-normal temperatures.
- freeze events: occurrences where ambient temperature falls at or below 0C, causing damage to vegetation, water infrastructure, or other temperature-sensitive systems.
- winter storms: synoptic-scale or mesoscale storm systems producing snow, ice, freezing rain, or high winds during the cold season.
- frost: the deposition of ice crystals on exposed surfaces when surface temperature falls below the freezing point, potentially damaging crops and vegetation.
- snowstorms: precipitation events producing significant snowfall accumulation that disrupts transportation, energy systems, and daily activities.
- ice storms: events characterized by freezing rain that deposits a glaze of ice on surfaces, causing damage to power lines, vegetation, and infrastructure.
- blizzards: severe winter storms with sustained high winds (>= 56 km/h), heavy snowfall, and reduced visibility (< 400 m) for a prolonged period.

Flood-related:
- floods: the temporary inundation of normally dry land by water from any source, with potential for loss of life, property damage, and ecosystem disruption.
- flash floods: rapid-onset flooding caused by intense precipitation, dam or levee failure, or sudden snowmelt, typically occurring within six hours of the causative event.
- river (fluvial) floods: inundation resulting from river discharge exceeding channel capacity due to sustained precipitation, snowmelt, or upstream flow accumulation.
- urban flooding: inundation in built environments when stormwater runoff exceeds the capacity of drainage infrastructure.
- coastal flooding: inundation of low-lying coastal areas by seawater driven by storm surge, wave action, tidal anomalies, or sea-level rise.
- compound flooding: flooding resulting from the co-occurrence or sequential occurrence of multiple drivers (e.g., precipitation coinciding with storm surge or elevated river stage).
- inundation: the submersion of land surfaces under water from any flooding mechanism.
- storm surge: an abnormal rise in sea level generated by a storm's wind stress and atmospheric pressure differential, often the primary cause of coastal flooding during tropical cyclones.
- heavy precipitation: rainfall or snowfall events of intensity or accumulation significantly exceeding climatological norms for a given region and duration.
- pluvial flooding: surface-water flooding caused by precipitation intensity exceeding local infiltration and drainage capacity, independent of fluvial or coastal processes.
- extreme precipitation: precipitation events in the upper tail of the regional intensity-duration-frequency distribution.

Drought-related:
- drought: a sustained period of below-normal precipitation or water availability that adversely affects agriculture, water supply, ecosystems, or human activities.
- water scarcity: insufficient freshwater availability to meet the demands of human, agricultural, industrial, or ecological systems.
- hydrological drought: a deficit in surface water and groundwater supply (streamflow, reservoir levels, aquifer recharge) relative to normal conditions.
- agricultural drought: a soil moisture deficit of sufficient magnitude and duration to adversely affect crop growth, development, or yield.
- meteorological drought: a sustained departure of precipitation below the climatological mean for a defined region and period.
- snow drought: anomalously low snowpack accumulation or premature snowmelt that reduces seasonal water availability in snowmelt-dependent basins.

Fire-related:
- wildfire: an unplanned, uncontrolled fire propagating through natural or modified vegetation, with potential to cause loss of life, property damage, air quality degradation, and ecosystem disruption.
- forest fire: wildfire burning predominantly in forested landscapes.
- bushfire: wildfire in shrubland or grassland vegetation (term prevalent in Australian usage).
- fire weather: meteorological conditions -- high temperature, low relative humidity, strong winds, and low fuel moisture -- that promote fire ignition, spread, and intensity.
- wildland fire: fire occurring in undeveloped or minimally developed vegetated landscapes.
- wildfire smoke: particulate-laden atmospheric emissions from wildfire combustion that degrade air quality and pose respiratory and cardiovascular health risks over wide areas.
- smoke exposure: human or ecological exposure to combustion-derived pollutants, particularly fine particulate matter (PM2.5), from wildfire or prescribed burning events.

Cyclonic storms:
- tropical cyclones: warm-core, low-pressure systems of organized convection originating over tropical or subtropical oceans, characterized by closed surface wind circulation, heavy precipitation, and storm surge.
- hurricanes: tropical cyclones in the North Atlantic, Caribbean, Gulf of Mexico, and eastern North Pacific basins with sustained winds >= 119 km/h (Saffir-Simpson scale).
- typhoons: tropical cyclones in the western North Pacific basin with sustained winds >= 119 km/h.
- cyclonic storms: rotating low-pressure weather systems, including tropical, subtropical, and extratropical cyclones.

Convective and wind hazards:
- convective storms: atmospheric disturbances driven by convective instability, often producing heavy precipitation, lightning, hail, damaging winds, or tornadoes.
- severe thunderstorms: convective systems meeting criteria for severe weather (e.g., hail >= 2.5 cm, wind gusts >= 93 km/h, or tornado production).
- hail: precipitation consisting of spheroidal or irregular ice particles formed in cumulonimbus updrafts, capable of damaging crops, vehicles, and structures.
- straight-line winds: non-tornadic damaging winds generated by thunderstorm outflow (downburst or gust front).
- downbursts: strong, localized downdrafts from a convective storm that produce damaging divergent winds at or near the surface.
- microbursts: downbursts with a horizontal extent of < 4 km, producing concentrated wind shear hazardous to aviation and ground structures.
- tornadoes: violently rotating columns of air extending from the base of a cumulonimbus cloud to the ground, characterized by a condensation funnel and capable of extreme localized destruction.
- extreme wind: non-convective or convective wind events of sufficient intensity to cause structural damage, infrastructure failure, or ecological disturbance.

Coastal and ocean hazards:
- sea-level rise: the long-term increase in mean sea level driven by thermal expansion of ocean water, land-ice mass loss, and changes in land water storage, increasing coastal flood and erosion risk.
- coastal erosion: the progressive loss of shoreline sediment and land area due to wave action, currents, storm impacts, and relative sea-level rise.
- salinization: the accumulation of soluble salts in soil or freshwater systems, often driven by seawater intrusion or altered hydrology, impairing agricultural productivity and water supply.
- saltwater intrusion: the landward migration of saline water into coastal freshwater aquifers or estuarine systems, degrading potable and irrigation water resources.
- ocean warming: the sustained increase in ocean heat content affecting marine ecosystems, atmospheric circulation, and global sea-level budgets.
- marine heatwaves: discrete, prolonged events of anomalously high sea-surface temperature that disrupt marine ecosystems (e.g., coral bleaching, species redistribution).
- ocean acidification: the reduction in seawater pH resulting from oceanic absorption of anthropogenic CO2, adversely affecting calcifying organisms and marine food webs.

Cryosphere hazards:
- sea-ice loss: the decline in areal extent, thickness, or seasonal duration of sea ice, affecting albedo feedbacks, marine ecosystems, and coastal exposure.
- glacial retreat and mass loss: the net reduction in glacier volume and extent due to warming, contributing to sea-level rise and altering downstream water supply.
- permafrost thaw: the degradation of perennially frozen ground in Arctic and subarctic regions, destabilizing infrastructure and releasing stored carbon as greenhouse gases.
- snowmelt timing shifts: changes in the seasonal onset or rate of snowmelt that alter streamflow timing, water availability, and flood risk.
- Arctic sea-ice change: long-term trends in Arctic sea-ice extent, volume, and seasonality with implications for global climate, ecosystems, and marine navigation.

Air quality hazards:
- air quality degradation: deterioration of ambient air quality due to elevated concentrations of criteria pollutants (PM2.5, PM10, O3, NO2, SO2, CO), with adverse public health and environmental effects.
- particulate matter (PM2.5): fine inhalable particles with aerodynamic diameter <= 2.5 um that penetrate the respiratory tract and circulatory system, associated with cardiovascular, respiratory, and mortality outcomes.
- dust storms: synoptic or mesoscale events in which strong winds entrain and transport large quantities of mineral dust, degrading air quality, visibility, and respiratory health, typically associated with arid or drought-affected regions.
- weather-amplified industrial pollution: industrial emission events whose atmospheric concentrations or dispersion are significantly modulated by meteorological or climatic conditions (e.g., temperature inversions, stagnation events).

Geomorphic and compound hazards:
- landslides and debris flows: the gravitational mass movement of rock, soil, or debris, often triggered by intense precipitation, rapid snowmelt, or seismic activity on saturated or destabilized slopes.
- subsidence: the gradual or sudden sinking of the ground surface due to groundwater withdrawal, permafrost thaw, or geological processes, exacerbating flood risk and infrastructure damage.

Impact, risk, and response contexts include:

- climate risk, weather risk, hazard exposure, vulnerability, resilience, adaptation, mitigation, preparedness, emergency response, disaster recovery -- all as defined above
- community impacts, social vulnerability, environmental justice, public health, mortality, morbidity, heat-related illness, smoke exposure, displacement (forced movement by a hazard), household or regional impacts
- infrastructure impacts or planning, including transportation, water systems, buildings, housing, communications, energy systems, public services, coastal assets, or urban systems
- ecosystem impacts, ecosystem services, forests, coasts, agriculture, water resources, biodiversity, natural-resource management, or climate-driven crop failure or yield loss
- planning, policy, governance (institutional arrangements for hazard risk), sustainability (long-term management under hazard conditions), disaster management, risk assessment, forecasting, early warning, decision support, or long-term adaptation strategies

Decision and score -- these **MUST agree**. Choose the score first, then set `decision` from it:

- score 3 -> decision "relevant": Strongly relevant. Climate/weather/environmental hazard topics or their societal/ecosystem implications are the paper's central focus.
- score 2 -> decision "relevant": Relevant. Hazard or impact/resilience content is clear and substantive but secondary to a broader topic (e.g., a multi-hazard review where one hazard is covered well, or a methods paper whose case study is a real hazard-impact context).
- score 1 -> decision "maybe": Partial or indirect. Some relevant content but thin, tangential, or underdeveloped. The paper touches on a relevant topic but it is not a meaningful part of the analysis.
- score 0 -> decision "irrelevant": No substantive climate/weather/environmental hazard or impact content.

**Never pair a high score with "irrelevant" or a 0 score with "relevant".**

Common false positives to mark IRRELEVANT unless they explicitly connect to a hazard or impact/resilience topic:

- Papers that mention climate or weather only as background motivation but focus on unrelated methods, materials, chemistry, biology, physics, or computing.
- General sustainability, energy, agriculture, urban, or public-health papers with no clear weather/climate/environmental hazard component.
- Biomedical, genomics, clinical, pharmaceutical, pathogen, spectroscopy, astrophysics, high-energy physics, fusion/plasma, semiconductor fabrication, nanomaterials, or chemical-engineering papers unrelated to hazards and impacts.
- Documents where terms such as "storm," "heat," "flood," "risk," "resilience," or "adaptation" are used metaphorically or in an unrelated technical sense.

Important judging guidance:

- Judge only from the provided evidence. Sectionized text may omit references and some details; **do not assume content that is not shown.**
- A document can be relevant even if it is about policy, governance, economics, health, ecosystems, or planning rather than physical hazard modeling, as long as the weather/climate/environmental hazard connection is clear.
- If the title/abstract are vague but multiple sections substantively discuss hazard or impact/resilience content, mark relevant.
- If only one weak sentence mentions the target topic and the rest is unrelated, mark irrelevant.
